UPSC Insights: The SHANTI Bill and India's Nuclear Future

Background

On the face of it, the Sustainable Harnessing and Advancement of Nuclear Energy for Transforming India Bill — the SHANTI Bill — passed by the Lok Sabha, appears to throw open the nuclear power sector to private participation. A closer reading of the legislation, however, suggests a more sobering reality.

The SHANTI Bill replaces both the Atomic Energy Act, 1962 and the Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage (CLND) Act, 2010. It is presented as ushering in two major reforms. First, it allows, for the first time in India, private companies to set up nuclear power plants. Second, it claims to remove the long-standing irritant of civil nuclear liability.

Key Issues

Ever since the CLND Act came into force, it has been blamed for scaring away private capital from India’s nuclear sector, with Section 17 singled out as the villain. Section 17(a) was contentious enough, as it allowed the plant operator to provide for a “right of recourse” against equipment suppliers. Section 17(b), however, drew the most criticism, even though it merely stated that the operator could seek damages from a supplier if an accident occurred as a consequence of an act of the supplier or its employees.

The SHANTI Bill eliminates this liability exposure for suppliers altogether, while capping the liability of the plant operator. In effect, it leaves the State — and ultimately citizens — to bear the burden of damages in the event of a nuclear accident, even as insurance-based solutions can be considered.

By removing the liability ‘thorn’, the government appears to be betting on a surge of private investment in nuclear power, especially amid renewed global interest in small modular reactors. That expectation seems optimistic, given that the government continues to retain complete control over the most critical element of the sector: nuclear fuel.

This is particularly disappointing at a time when new fuel technologies are emerging. An Indian-owned US company, Clean Core Thorium, for instance, has developed a fuel that could allow India to enter the thorium cycle immediately. Its ANEEL (Advanced Nuclear Energy for Enriched Life) fuel has been successfully tested for burn-up at the Idaho National Laboratory in the US. Several variants of high-assay low-enriched uranium (HALEU) fuels are also now available globally. Yet the SHANTI Bill effectively denies private plant operators access to fuel technologies. In any case, vesting full control of the fuel cycle in the government is an open invitation to rent-seeking. Proliferation concerns are real, but they are better addressed through robust safeguards and oversight.

Analysis

The SHANTI Bill represents a mixed bag of reforms. While it ostensibly opens the nuclear power sector to private players and addresses the long-standing issue of supplier liability, it simultaneously introduces new challenges and maintains significant government control. The shift of accident liability to the State and citizens, coupled with the government's continued monopoly over nuclear fuel, suggests that the 'private participation' envisioned might be more limited than initially perceived. The lack of access for private operators to emerging fuel technologies stifles innovation and limits the potential for a truly transformative shift in India's nuclear energy landscape. While proliferation is a legitimate concern, achieving a balance between national security interests and fostering a vibrant, competitive private sector is crucial.

Constitutional / Governance Angle

One clear positive in the Bill is the grant of statutory backing to the Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB), making it accountable directly to Parliament. At present, the AERB reports to the Atomic Energy Commission. Regulatory independence is a welcome reform, aligning with principles of good governance and institutional accountability. However, this independence will matter in practice only if there are multiple players for the regulator to oversee. The shifting of nuclear accident liability to the State and citizens also raises questions about the state's welfare responsibility and the equitable distribution of risk, which are key governance considerations. Furthermore, the potential for rent-seeking due to government control over the fuel cycle highlights the need for transparent and robust oversight mechanisms.

Why it matters for UPSC

This topic is highly relevant for UPSC aspirants across multiple General Studies (GS) Papers:

  • GS Paper 3: Science & Technology, Economy (Infrastructure, Investment)
    • Science & Technology: Understanding nuclear energy policy, indigenous nuclear program, emerging fuel technologies (thorium cycle, SMRs, HALEU), and the role of regulatory bodies like AERB.
    • Economy: Analysis of private sector participation in infrastructure, foreign direct investment, challenges to industrial growth, and energy security.
  • GS Paper 2: Governance, Polity (Legislation)
    • Governance: Regulatory reforms (AERB's statutory backing, independence, accountability), public policy-making, liability laws, public welfare, and the balance between state control and private enterprise.
    • Polity: Understanding the legislative process, analysis of new bills replacing existing acts, and their constitutional implications.
  • Essay Paper: Themes related to energy security, sustainable development, public-private partnerships, and regulatory governance can be drawn from this topic.

Way Forward

To truly unleash the potential of India's nuclear sector and attract significant private investment, a more balanced approach is needed. This includes:

  • Revisiting Fuel Cycle Control: While national security is paramount, exploring models that allow limited private participation in fuel processing or access to alternative fuel technologies under stringent international and national safeguards could spur innovation and efficiency.
  • Robust Liability Framework: Developing comprehensive, market-based insurance solutions for nuclear liability, potentially involving a pool of insurers, could better protect all stakeholders – suppliers, operators, the State, and citizens – from the financial burden of an accident.
  • Strengthening AERB: While statutory backing is a positive step, ensuring AERB has adequate resources, technical expertise, and operational autonomy to effectively regulate a diverse and growing nuclear sector is crucial.
  • Incentivizing Research & Development: Actively promoting indigenous R&D in nuclear technologies, including advanced reactor designs and fuel cycles, and creating an ecosystem where private players can contribute to this innovation.
  • Clarity and Predictability: Ensuring a clear and predictable policy and regulatory environment will be essential to attract sustained private and foreign investment in this capital-intensive sector.

Background

The Sustainable Harnessing and Advancement of Nuclear Energy for Transforming India Bill (SHANTI Bill), passed by the Lok Sabha, aims to reform India's nuclear power sector.

Key Issues

  • Private Participation: The Bill allows private companies to set up nuclear power plants for the first time in India.
  • Civil Nuclear Liability: It replaces the Atomic Energy Act, 1962 and the Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage (CLND) Act, 2010.
  • Supplier Liability: The SHANTI Bill eliminates liability exposure for equipment suppliers, which was a contentious issue under Section 17 of the CLND Act, 2010.
  • Operator Liability: The liability of the plant operator is capped.
  • State Burden: The State and citizens may bear the burden of damages in case of a nuclear accident.
  • Nuclear Fuel Control: The government retains complete control over nuclear fuel, denying private operators access to new fuel technologies like thorium-based fuel (e.g., Clean Core Thorium's ANEEL fuel) and HALEU fuels.
  • Regulatory Independence: The Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB) is granted statutory backing and made accountable directly to Parliament.

Analysis

While the SHANTI Bill signals an opening of the nuclear sector to private players and seeks to address concerns over civil nuclear liability, its impact is tempered by significant limitations.

  • The removal of supplier liability is intended to attract private investment but shifts the ultimate risk to the public.
  • Retaining government control over nuclear fuel restricts the adoption of advanced and potentially more efficient fuel technologies. This could hinder the growth and diversification of India's nuclear program.
  • The grant of statutory backing to AERB is a positive step towards regulatory independence. However, its effectiveness will depend on the presence of multiple operators for it to oversee.
  • Concerns about proliferation are acknowledged, but the Bill's approach to fuel control might be less effective than robust safeguards and oversight.

Constitutional / Governance Angle

  • The Bill potentially impacts the principles of **public accountability** and **risk allocation** within the energy sector.
  • The **statutory independence of the AERB** directly to Parliament enhances **institutional governance** and **oversight**.
  • The concentration of control over nuclear fuel in government hands raises questions about **economic liberalization** and **competitive markets**.

Why it matters for UPSC

This development is crucial for UPSC aspirants and can be linked to:

  • GS Paper I: Society - Impact of industrial development and policy on public safety.
  • GS Paper II: Governance - Regulatory bodies, accountability mechanisms, policy formulation and implementation.
  • GS Paper II: Government Policies and Interventions - Energy policy, economic reforms, public-private partnerships.
  • GS Paper III: Economy - Infrastructure, industrial policy, investment in critical sectors.
  • GS Paper III: Science and Technology - Nuclear energy, emerging technologies, fuel cycles.
  • GS Paper III: Security Challenges - Nuclear proliferation concerns, safeguards.

Way Forward

  • Develop robust insurance mechanisms and compensation frameworks to effectively manage the risks associated with nuclear accidents, even with capped operator liability.
  • Explore options for private sector participation in the nuclear fuel cycle, alongside stringent regulatory oversight and safeguards, to foster innovation and leverage new fuel technologies.
  • Ensure the AERB, now with statutory backing, is adequately resourced and empowered to effectively regulate a potentially expanding nuclear sector with diverse players.
  • Foster transparency and public engagement regarding the risks and benefits of nuclear energy and the implications of the SHANTI Bill.

Background

The Sustainable Harnessing and Advancement of Nuclear Energy for Transforming India Bill (SHANTI Bill), passed by the Lok Sabha, aims to reform India's nuclear power sector.

It replaces the Atomic Energy Act, 1962 and the Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage (CLND) Act, 2010.

Key Issues

  • Private Participation: Allows private companies to set up nuclear power plants for the first time.
  • Civil Nuclear Liability: Aims to remove the perceived obstacle of civil nuclear liability for private investors.
  • Supplier Liability: The SHANTI Bill eliminates liability exposure for equipment suppliers.
  • Operator Liability: Liability of the plant operator is capped.
  • State/Citizen Burden: The State, and ultimately citizens, may bear the burden of damages in case of an accident.
  • Fuel Control: Government retains complete control over nuclear fuel, denying private operators access to new fuel technologies.
  • Regulatory Independence: Grants statutory backing to the Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB), making it accountable to Parliament.

Analysis

While the Bill opens the sector to private players, it shifts the ultimate burden of accident damages to the State/citizens by removing supplier liability and capping operator liability.

The government's expectation of a surge in private investment may be optimistic due to continued government control over nuclear fuel, a critical element.

New fuel technologies, such as those developed by Clean Core Thorium (ANEEL fuel) and globally available HALEU fuels, are inaccessible to private operators.

Vesting full control of the fuel cycle in the government is seen as an invitation to rent-seeking, with proliferation concerns better addressed through robust safeguards.

The statutory backing of the AERB is a positive step towards regulatory independence, but its practical impact depends on having multiple entities for the regulator to oversee.

Constitutional / Governance Angle

The Bill raises questions about the allocation of risk and responsibility in a high-stakes sector. The shift of liability burden to the State has implications for public finances and citizen welfare.

The grant of statutory backing to the AERB strengthens its institutional framework and accountability, aligning with principles of good governance and independent oversight.

Why it matters for UPSC

  • GS Paper I: Society - Impact of policy on citizen safety and financial burden.
  • GS Paper II: Governance - Regulatory independence, accountability of statutory bodies, role of the state vs. private sector.
  • GS Paper II: Polity - Legislative process, parliamentary oversight.
  • GS Paper III: Economy - Private sector participation, investment in strategic sectors.
  • GS Paper III: Science and Technology - Nuclear energy, fuel cycle technology, regulatory frameworks.
  • GS Paper III: Security - Nuclear proliferation concerns, safeguards.

Way Forward

  • Implement robust safeguards and oversight mechanisms to address proliferation concerns effectively.
  • Re-evaluate government control over nuclear fuel to allow private sector access to emerging fuel technologies, fostering innovation.
  • Explore insurance-based solutions to manage nuclear liability effectively without placing undue burden on the State and citizens.
  • Ensure the effective functioning of the independently regulated AERB by fostering a competitive and diverse nuclear power sector.

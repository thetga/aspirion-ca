Of pilots and other drivers

Mon Dec 22 2025

Background

Since the mid-1930s, commercial aviation has seen pilots consistently advocating for reduced duty times. Historically, airlines have often conceded to pilot demands due to the prohibitive costs associated with grounded aircraft. Unlike pilots, who have resorted to mass sick leaves, IndiGo pilots have not engaged in strikes, despite their large numbers (around 5,000) and the airline's extensive fleet (over 400 aircraft).

Key Issues

  • Pilot Overwork: IndiGo pilots report being overworked, a situation acknowledged as not new to the aviation industry.
  • Truck and Train Driver Conditions: Truck and train drivers face significantly more demanding working conditions, including longer hours, lower pay, and harsher environments compared to pilots.
  • Lack of Attention: Despite the severe overwork and stress faced by truck and train drivers, they receive little public or media attention.
  • Market Structure Impact: The economic and commercial considerations within the transport industry, influenced by market structures (perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly), contribute to the issue of overworked labor.
  • High Capital-Labor Ratio: Across all transport segments (trucking, railways, airlines), a high capital-labor ratio is maintained to achieve operational goals, inherently leading to overworked drivers and pilots.

Analysis

The situation at IndiGo, while concerning, highlights a broader systemic issue within India's transport sector. The focus on pilots, often perceived as high-earning professionals, overshadows the plight of truck and train drivers who endure far more arduous conditions and lower remuneration. The author argues that the "public" outcry regarding IndiGo is limited to a vocal, upper-middle-class segment that is less concerned with the more prevalent and dangerous dysfunctions in road and railway systems. The economic models employed by different transport sectors, from perfect competition in trucking to monopoly in railways and oligopoly in airlines, all converge on a strategy of maximizing efficiency through understaffing, leading to inherent labor overwork.

The article criticizes the selective attention given to certain professions, suggesting that the economic imperatives of the transport industry prioritize keeping the capital-labor ratio high, which necessitates flogging existing labor. This leads to a perpetual shortage of operating staff and a cycle of exhaustion for drivers and pilots.

Constitutional / Governance Angle

While not explicitly detailed in the provided text, the working conditions of truck and train drivers can be examined through the lens of:

  • Labour Laws: Provisions related to working hours, minimum wages, and occupational safety.
  • Regulatory Bodies: The role of government agencies in overseeing transport operations and ensuring fair labor practices.
  • Public Policy: The government's responsibility to address systemic issues affecting essential service providers and their workforce.
  • Social Justice: Ensuring equitable treatment and decent working conditions for all segments of the workforce, regardless of their visibility or perceived status.

Way Forward

  • Policy Interventions: Government policies need to address the root causes of overwork in the transport sector, moving beyond symbolic gestures.
  • Regulatory Enforcement: Stricter enforcement of labor laws and safety regulations for truck and train drivers.
  • Economic Re-evaluation: Reassessing the capital-labor ratio strategies in transport companies to ensure sustainable working conditions.
  • Public Awareness: Raising public awareness about the critical roles and challenging conditions faced by truck and train drivers.
  • Industry Reforms: Encouraging industry players to adopt more humane labor practices and invest in better working conditions and job security for their operating staff.

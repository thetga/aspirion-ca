FDI relaxations alone will not attract insurance players

Sun Dec 21 2025

Background

The **Sabka Bima Sabki Raksha (Amendment of Insurance Laws) Act** aims to simplify Indiaâ€™s insurance laws and improve the ease of doing business. It integrates three key acts: the Insurance Act 1938, the LIC Act 1956, and the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority Act 1999 into a single legislation. This move seeks to create a **level playing field** between private and public sector insurers, as well as Indian and foreign-owned insurers, by removing special treatment for the Life Insurance Corporation.

Key Issues

  • Integration of Insurance Laws: Consolidating multiple acts into one for simplification.
  • Level Playing Field: Ensuring equal opportunities for all types of insurers.
  • Enhanced IRDAI Powers: Granting the regulator stronger enforcement and investigative capabilities.
  • Increased FDI Limits: Raising the foreign direct investment cap in insurance ventures to 100%.
  • Relaxed Management Control Stipulations: Removing the requirement for majority Indian Board members and top executives in foreign-owned ventures.
  • Challenges in Insurance Penetration and Profitability: Addressing issues like life insurance being a "push product" and profitability struggles in general insurance.

Analysis

The Act introduces significant regulatory and structural changes. The expansion of IRDAI's powers, including the ability to order searches, seizures, investigations, and disgorgement of wrongful gains, similar to SEBI, is a major step towards **strengthening policyholder protection**. The increased FDI limit to 100% and the relaxation of management control requirements are designed to attract more foreign capital and expertise. However, the text raises concerns about whether these measures will be effective given the current market conditions:

  • Consumer Perception: Life insurance in India is often seen as a push product rather than a voluntary purchase for protection.
  • Product Mix: A significant portion of life insurance sales comprises fixed-return plans, not pure protection.
  • General Insurance Profitability: Regulated tariffs (especially in motor insurance) and underwriting losses in health and general insurance due to mispriced risk hinder profitability.
  • Policyholder Experience: There's a disconnect between patchy industry profitability and a sub-par policyholder experience regarding premiums and claims.

The analysis suggests that without fundamental changes in consumer perceptions and industry business models, attracting significant FDI and improving the insurance sector's health might be challenging.

Constitutional / Governance Angle

This amendment falls under the purview of **Parliamentary legislation** concerning financial services and regulation. The enhancement of the IRDAI's powers represents a significant aspect of **regulatory governance**, empowering an independent statutory body to enforce rules and protect consumer interests. The changes in FDI policy reflect the government's approach to **economic governance** and its strategy for capital inflow and sector development. The focus on policyholder protection underscores the **accountability and responsiveness** of the governance framework to citizens' financial well-being.

Way Forward

  • Effective Implementation of IRDAI Powers: The regulator needs to actively and effectively utilize its enhanced powers to address malpractices and protect policyholders.
  • Market-Driven Tariffs: Liberalizing tariffs, especially in motor insurance, to allow market forces to dictate pricing and improve insurer profitability.
  • Robust Underwriting Practices: Encouraging and enforcing the adoption of data-based underwriting to accurately price risks.
  • Consumer Education and Awareness: Shifting the perception of insurance from a push product to a product of voluntary purchase for genuine protection.
  • Innovation in Products: Developing and promoting insurance products that truly meet protection needs rather than fixed-return plans.
  • Monitoring FDI Inflow: Closely tracking the impact of increased FDI limits and adapting policies to encourage genuine investment and improve sector performance.

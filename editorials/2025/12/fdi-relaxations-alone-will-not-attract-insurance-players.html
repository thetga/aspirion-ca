UPSC Insights: Sabka Bima Sabki Raksha (Amendment of Insurance Laws) Act

Background

The recently passed Sabka Bima Sabki Raksha (Amendment of Insurance Laws) Act is a significant legislative effort aimed at simplifying India’s insurance laws and enhancing the ease of doing business in the sector. The Act brings about comprehensive changes on two primary fronts. Firstly, it integrates the existing Insurance Act 1938, the LIC Act 1956, and the IRDAI Act 1999 into a single, unified piece of legislation. This consolidation is designed to create a level playing field for all insurers, whether private or public, Indian or foreign-owned, by doing away with the special treatment previously accorded to the Life Insurance Corporation. Secondly, recognizing the historical disadvantage faced by policyholders, the Act significantly expands the regulatory powers of the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI) to better protect their interests.

Key Issues

Expanded Powers of IRDAI

  • IRDAI is now empowered to order searches, seizures, and inspections of insurers.
  • It can investigate intermediaries and mandate the disgorgement of wrongful gains made by insurers or intermediaries, mirroring the powers of SEBI.
  • The authority can impose penalties of up to ₹10 crore on insurers and establish regulatory caps on commissions paid to agents.
  • Furthermore, IRDAI can supersede the Board of an insurer and appoint an Administrator to safeguard policyholder interests, demonstrating a robust commitment to consumer protection.

Relaxation of Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) Limits

  • The Act’s most discussed provision is the relaxation of FDI limits in the insurance sector, allowing foreign players to own up to 100% equity in Indian insurance ventures, up from the previous limit of 74%.
  • This move is premised on the belief that capital constraints have hampered industry growth and insurance penetration in India.
  • Notably, the previous hike from 49% to 74% in March 2021 garnered a lukewarm response, with only a handful of licensed insurers witnessing an increase in foreign stakes.
  • A significant barrier, the stipulation requiring foreign-owned insurers to have a majority of Indian Board members and top executives, has been removed. Now, only the Chairperson/MD/CEO needs to be an Indian resident, granting foreign promoters greater management control.

Analysis

While the expanded powers endow IRDAI with considerable regulatory muscle, their actual impact will depend on the regulator's willingness and effectiveness in flexing them. The relaxation of FDI limits to 100% is a bold step, but its success in attracting substantial foreign investment remains uncertain.

Despite critical events like Covid-19, life insurance in India largely remains a "push product," often hard-sold rather than voluntarily purchased for protection. Unlike global trends where life covers primarily protect dependents, the majority of life insurance sales in India comprise traditional fixed-return plans with minimal protection. In the general insurance segment, insurers face challenges in sustaining profitable operations due to regulated tariffs (especially in the motor business) and significant underwriting losses in health and general insurance, often stemming from mispriced risks. This situation is further complicated by the puzzling coexistence of patchy industry profitability and sub-par policyholder experiences regarding premiums and claims. Unless fundamental shifts occur in consumer perceptions and industry operating models, global players might remain disinclined to invest further, even with increased ownership flexibility.

Constitutional / Governance Angle

The passage of the Sabka Bima Sabki Raksha Act by Parliament underscores the legislative authority in regulating the financial sector, specifically insurance, which falls under the Union List (Entry 47, Insurance) of the Seventh Schedule of the Constitution. From a governance perspective, the Act aims to establish a level playing field, a core principle of good governance, and enhances the autonomy and enforcement powers of IRDAI as a statutory regulator. The expanded powers of IRDAI, akin to SEBI, reflect a move towards strengthening regulatory oversight and market integrity. This legislative intervention also implicitly addresses the Directive Principles of State Policy by promoting consumer protection and welfare through stronger regulatory mechanisms for policyholders.

Why it matters for UPSC

  • GS Paper 3: Indian Economy and issues relating to Planning, Mobilization of Resources, Growth, Development and Employment.
    • Financial Sector Reforms: The Act represents a significant reform in the insurance sector, a crucial component of the Indian financial system.
    • FDI Policy: Changes in FDI limits in a key sector like insurance directly relate to government policy on foreign investment, its impact on capital formation, and economic growth.
    • Role of Regulatory Bodies: Analysis of IRDAI's powers and its effectiveness is vital for understanding financial sector governance.
    • Insurance Penetration and Financial Inclusion: The Act's aims touch upon increasing insurance penetration, a key aspect of financial inclusion.
    • Ease of Doing Business: Simplification of laws and creating a level playing field are directly linked to government efforts in improving India's business environment.
  • GS Paper 2: Governance, Constitution, Polity, Social Justice and International Relations.
    • Regulatory Bodies: Understanding the mandate, powers, and functioning of statutory bodies like IRDAI.
    • Legislative Process: The Act highlights the role of Parliament in enacting laws for economic governance.
    • Consumer Protection: The focus on safeguarding policyholder interests falls under social justice and citizen welfare.

Way Forward

To truly realize the objectives of the Sabka Bima Sabki Raksha Act, several measures are crucial:

  • Effective Regulatory Enforcement: IRDAI must actively and judiciously utilize its enhanced powers to ensure market discipline, protect policyholders, and curb wrongful gains by insurers and intermediaries.
  • Market-Driven Tariffs: To improve the profitability of general insurers, especially in segments like motor insurance, tariffs need to be gradually deregulated and left to market forces.
  • Data-Based Underwriting: The institution of robust, data-driven underwriting practices is essential to accurately price risk and reduce underwriting losses, thereby improving industry profitability.
  • Shifting Consumer Perceptions: Efforts are needed to educate policyholders and shift their perception of life insurance from being primarily an investment tool to a vital protection mechanism.
  • Evolving Industry Models: Indian insurance companies must adapt their business models to address the challenges of profitability and enhance the overall policyholder experience in terms of premiums and claims settlement.
  • Monitoring FDI Impact: Continuous monitoring of the actual capital infusion and market dynamics post-100% FDI will be crucial to assess the policy's effectiveness.

Background

The Sabka Bima Sabki Raksha (Amendment of Insurance Laws) Act, recently passed by Parliament, aims to simplify India's insurance laws and enhance the ease of doing business.

It introduces changes on two main fronts:

  • Integration of Laws: It consolidates the Insurance Act 1938, LIC Act 1956, and IRDA Act 1999 into a single legislation.
  • Level Playing Field: It establishes a balanced environment for private and public sector insurers, as well as Indian and foreign-owned entities, by removing special treatment for the Life Insurance Corporation.

Key Issues

  • Policyholder Protection: The Act seeks to address the disadvantages faced by policyholders.
  • Regulatory Powers: It significantly expands the powers of the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDAI).
  • Foreign Direct Investment (FDI): The Act allows foreign players to own up to 100 per cent equity in Indian insurance ventures, an increase from the previous 74 per cent.
  • Management Control: The stipulation for majority Indian Board members and top executives in foreign-owned insurers has been removed. Now, only the Chairperson/MD/CEO needs to be an Indian resident, granting foreign promoters greater management control.
  • Industry Profitability: Challenges remain in achieving sustained profitability for insurers, particularly in general insurance.
  • Consumer Perception: Life insurance in India is largely a "push product" with many sales driven by traditional fixed-return plans rather than genuine protection needs.

Analysis

The integration of insurance laws and the establishment of a level playing field are positive steps towards modernization. The expansion of IRDAI's powers, including the ability to order searches, seizures, inspections, investigate intermediaries, and levy penalties up to â‚¹10 crore, provides the regulator with significant enforcement capabilities. This mirrors the powers of SEBI.

The relaxation of FDI limits to 100% aims to attract capital and boost insurance penetration. However, the lukewarm response to the previous hike in FDI from 49% to 74% in March 2021 suggests that other factors might be at play. The removal of the requirement for a majority Indian Board and executive team could make Indian ventures more attractive to foreign investors.

Despite these measures, the inherent challenges in the Indian insurance market, such as the nature of product sales and profitability issues in general insurance, may continue to deter significant FDI. Addressing these underlying issues is crucial for the Act's ultimate success in attracting foreign investment and improving insurance penetration.

Constitutional / Governance Angle

The Act falls under the purview of:

  • Legislative Powers: Parliament's authority to legislate on matters related to insurance and financial regulation.
  • Executive Authority: The role of the government in implementing economic reforms and simplifying business processes.
  • Regulatory Governance: The strengthening of IRDAI as an independent regulatory body to ensure market integrity and policyholder protection.

The Act empowers a statutory body (IRDAI) with enhanced enforcement and supervisory mechanisms, reflecting a move towards robust regulatory governance.

Why it matters for UPSC

This Act is relevant for multiple sections of the UPSC Civil Services Exam:

  • GS Paper I: Economy - Insurance sector reforms, FDI, financial inclusion, economic development.
  • GS Paper II: Governance - Regulatory bodies, policy formulation, ease of doing business, public administration.
  • GS Paper III: Economy - Financial sector, capital markets, foreign investment, challenges to economic growth.

Understanding the nuances of insurance sector reforms, their economic implications, and the role of regulatory bodies is crucial for both Prelims and Mains examinations.

Way Forward

  • Effective Implementation: IRDAI needs to effectively utilize its enhanced powers to ensure fair practices and policyholder protection.
  • Market-Driven Pricing: For general insurance, moving towards market-driven tariffs and robust data-based underwriting is essential to improve profitability.
  • Consumer Awareness: Efforts should be made to shift consumer perception towards genuine protection needs rather than solely focusing on investment returns.
  • Attracting FDI: While FDI limits are relaxed, further steps might be needed to address the underlying structural issues that affect the attractiveness of the Indian insurance market for foreign investors.
  • Monitoring Impact: Continuous monitoring of the Act's impact on insurance penetration, consumer experience, and industry growth is necessary.

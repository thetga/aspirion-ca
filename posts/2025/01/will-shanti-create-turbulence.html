SHANTI Bill: Navigating Turbulence in India's Nuclear Energy Future

January 10, 2025 · Editorial
Source: The Hindu
Governance

The SHANTI Bill, 2025: Modernizing India's Nuclear Landscape

The Sustainable Harnessing and Advancement of Nuclear Energy for Transforming India (SHANTI) Bill, 2025, passed by Parliament, aims to overhaul India's nuclear legal framework. It permits limited private participation in reactor construction, ownership, operation, and decommissioning, while strengthening the Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB) through statutory recognition.

The policy rationale centers on meeting the ambitious goal of achieving 100 GW of nuclear energy capacity by 2047 and advancing decarbonization targets by 2070. The bill also allocates ₹20,000 crore for a dedicated nuclear energy mission focusing on Small Modular Reactors (SMRs), aiming for five indigenously developed SMRs by 2033.

Key Concerns and Policy Implications

While facilitating energy transition, the implementation of SHANTI raises significant policy questions regarding infrastructure readiness, cost management, and regulatory oversight:

  • Impact on Coal Sector: Increased nuclear capacity deployment is expected to accelerate the retirement of high-tariff, non-pithead coal plants. Repurposing retired coal sites for nuclear facilities could streamline infrastructure integration by utilizing existing transmission corridors.
  • Centralized Tariff Mechanism: Clause 37(1) grants the Central Government the authority to fix nuclear power tariffs based on a specified formula, potentially overriding existing provisions of the Electricity Act, 2003. Transparency in this formula, ensuring it covers lifecycle costs (fuel, waste management, decommissioning), is crucial for investor confidence.
  • Execution and Cost Risks: Nuclear projects involve extremely high capital costs (₹16-20 crore per MW) and long gestation periods. Private sector participation is tempered by a lack of operational track record and concerns over execution risks.
  • Operator Liability: The bill caps operator liability at ₹3,000 crore and removes recourse for equipment suppliers, aligning with global norms but potentially creating challenges regarding accountability for accidents or failures, especially given nascent waste management frameworks.

Way Forward

Successful implementation of the SHANTI framework requires proactive governance to mitigate inherent sectoral risks:

  • Regulatory Clarity: The government must immediately notify the exact mechanism and formula for tariff determination to ensure predictability for investors and States opting for power offtake.
  • Risk Sharing: Clear frameworks must be established for waste management, decommissioning liabilities, and addressing the operational risks assumed by private entities lacking prior experience.
  • Grid Flexibility: Policy must incentivize energy storage solutions and demand flexibility options to manage the baseload contribution from nuclear power effectively without leading to curtailment of other renewable resources.

Prelims Pointers

  • Target Capacity: India aims for 100 GW of nuclear energy capacity by 2047.
  • SMR Mission Funding: Allocation of ₹20,000 crore towards the development and deployment of indigenous Small Modular Reactors (SMRs).
  • Regulatory Body: The Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB) receives statutory recognition under the SHANTI Bill.

Mains Conclusion

The SHANTI Bill represents a necessary legislative modernization designed to unlock private capital and accelerate India's clean energy trajectory. However, the success of this transition hinges not just on capacity targets, but on establishing robust, transparent mechanisms for cost recovery and liability management, thereby ensuring that the pursuit of clean energy does not create unforeseen economic or regulatory turbulence for consumers and the power sector.

Analysis of the Implications of the SHANTI Bill on India's Nuclear Energy Sector

10 January 2025 · Editorial
Source: The Hindu
Polity Economy Governance

SHANTI Bill: A Pivotal Step in India's Nuclear Journey

The Sustainable Harnessing and Advancement of Nuclear Energy for Transforming India (SHANTI) Bill, 2025, has been passed by Parliament, marking a significant shift in India's nuclear legal framework. The bill aims to modernize regulations, permit limited private participation, and grant statutory recognition to the Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB).

The primary rationale is to support India’s clean energy transition and achieve the ambitious goal of 100 GW of nuclear energy capacity by 2047. Private companies can now build, own, operate, and decommission reactors, including Small Modular Reactors (SMRs), previously the domain of NPCIL. The government has also allocated ₹20,000 crore for an SMR mission targeting five indigenous SMRs by 2033.

Implications for the Electricity Sector

  • Coal Plant Retirement: Increased nuclear capacity is expected to accelerate the retirement of older, high-tariff coal plants. Existing transmission infrastructure at retired sites could be repurposed for new nuclear plants, easing grid integration costs.
  • Centralised Tariff Mechanism: Clause 37(1) of SHANTI grants the Central Government the power to fix nuclear power tariffs based on a specified formula, overriding aspects of the Electricity Act, 2003. While intended to provide price visibility for investors, transparency in the tariff formula is crucial, as it must account for high lifecycle costs (capital, waste management, decommissioning).
  • Cost and Execution Risks: Nuclear projects involve extremely high capital costs (₹16-20 crore per MW) and long gestation periods. Private sector participation raises concerns regarding execution risk, given their lack of operational track record in this domain.
  • Operator Liability: Operator liability has been capped at a graded amount, up to ₹3,000 crore, removing recourse for equipment suppliers. Critics question the management of potential large-scale damages under this structure.

While the SHANTI Bill provides a modern legal scaffolding for nuclear expansion, its success hinges on clear implementation rules, transparent tariff determination, and states' willingness to commit to offtake agreements for the generated power. The interplay between nuclear generation, storage, and demand flexibility will ultimately determine system costs.
